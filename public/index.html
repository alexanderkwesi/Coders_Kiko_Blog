Your guide to understanding Kiko African English expressions and cultural nuances - A coders delight or worst nightmare?

In Kiko, "I'm coming" often doesn't mean the speaker is immediately on their way. It's a cultural expression that can mean:

  • I'll be there soon (but not necessarily right now)
  • I acknowledge your request and will attend to it
  • I'm about to start getting ready to come

This differs from standard English where "I'm coming" implies immediate action. The Kiko version is more about intention than imminent arrival.

The progressive form "I'm hearing you" is commonly used in Kiko to indicate active, ongoing attention and understanding. This construction:

  • Emphasizes the present moment of comprehension
  • Often conveys empathy and engagement with the speaker
  • May derive from similar structures in African languages

While "I hear you" might sound more natural in standard English, "I'm hearing you" carries additional cultural weight in Kiko contexts, suggesting not just auditory reception but emotional connection.

In Nigerian English and other Kiko varieties, "sorry" serves multiple functions beyond apology:

  • Empathy expression: "Sorry oh!" shows sympathy for someone's situation
  • Attention getter: Repeated "sorries" can preface an important statement
  • Conversation smoother: Used to maintain harmony in dialogue
  • Shock response: "Sorry! Sorry!! Sorry!!!" reacts to surprising news

The tonal delivery and repetition pattern change the meaning completely from the standard English apology.

The phrase "you people" in Kiko has distinct usages that differ from standard English:

Context Kiko Meaning Standard English Equivalent
"You people are not serious" General address to a group, often affectionate "You all aren't serious"
"Come and see you people" Drawing attention to the group's actions "Come see what you all are doing"

Unlike in standard English where "you people" can sound accusatory or distancing, in Kiko it's typically neutral or even endearing, reflecting the communal nature of many African societies.

Kiko greetings often go beyond simple hellos to encompass wellbeing inquiries and community connection:

"How now?"

(Nigeria) - Literal translation from local languages meaning "How are things?"

"You woke up well?"

(East Africa) - Morning greeting emphasizing health and safety

"How body?"

(West Africa) - Pidgin inquiry about physical wellbeing

"Are you there?"

(Southern Africa) - Phone greeting checking connection and presence

These greetings reflect cultural values that prioritize personal connection and community wellbeing over transactional communication.
